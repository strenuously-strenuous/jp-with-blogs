A gap year after 40

22nd September 2023

It is seldom that I get a catch in my throat before having to travel. But today, I am wandering around with pain in my legs, constriction in my heart, and a churning stomach. It is the first time that I will be separated from my daughters for three long months. That is to say, away from my two oldest girls as the youngest and I will be travelling together in India during this time.

It brings me to think about the gap year that I never had. In my time, not many people had the habit to taking a year to go out and explore the world, to really contemplate what you wanted to study next, to broaden your outlook and develop yourself before jumping in the deep end for another four years. And while, back then, almost no one travelled (and certainly not with their mother) during a gap year, my youngest was eager to have this experience together.

And so I’m taking a gap year after the age of 40. I believe that a “midlife crisis” should also count as a reason for a sort of “gap year” of three months. At my age, I am still standing at the same point — more or less — where I stood before I undertook my studies: Who in Heaven’s name am I?

Let’s face it: when I look in the mirror, I no longer see that image that I admired for the last few decades, and there’s no facial treatment that can do anything about ageing — even though commercials tell you otherwise. Moreover, the very nature of my motherhood is changing into something that is completely unknown for me, and I start to get short of breath when I realise that my girls will be living with me for only a few more years — perhaps less, even — before they head out into the wide world.

What then? What will I do? Who am I then? What will my life look like? I can’t make a single prediction about what my life will be like without my three girls in the house. Already for twenty-one years, I haven’t known anything except having them always here.

But that also supplies to them. My enthusiasm over the fact that they will have a three-month trial run of living together on their own is mixed with the stress of knowing that they only have each other to rely on: I won’t be there to make their lunch before they head off to university; in the evenings, there won’t be a pot of food ready for them on the stove; there won’t be an afternoon tea moment where we talk about our days…the laundry won’t magically pick itself up off the floor or be ironed, the fridge won’t fill itself, and the car won’t drive itself here and there…

They, too, are used to having me around all the time. As a mother, this is a transition from watching over my “little chickens” to helping them prepare themselves for their own, independent life. After this trip, I will share things with them in a very different manner — they’ll be my peers. And my little girls will be upstanding, independent women of whom I am proud. And for that, my gap year, even on this later age, will absolutely be helpful to have this transition go smoothly.
